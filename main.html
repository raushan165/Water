WATER AVAILABILITY

Water availability is a fundamental cornerstone of life on Earth, intricately intertwined with the health of ecosystems, human well-being, and economic development. As our planet faces increasing pressures from climate change, population growth, and urbanization, ensuring reliable access to clean water has become a paramount challenge. From sustaining agriculture to supporting industries and meeting the needs of growing urban populations, the availability and quality of water resources are pivotal for a sustainable future. This crucial resource underpins not only environmental sustainability but also social equity and economic prosperity.

Ratio Of Water Avilable In Different Continents

The ratios of fresh water and water availability

  • ASIA

    28%
    Asia is home to more than half of the world’s population and has less freshwater than any continent other than Antarctica. The water availability in Asia is measured at 3,920 cubic meters per person per year. Asia has approximately 28 percent of the world’s total freshwater resources.

  • AFRICA

    9%
    Africa is the world´s second-driest continent after Australia. About 66% of Africa is arid or semi-arid and more than 300 of the 800 million people in sub-Saharan Africa live in a water-scarce environment – meaning that they have less than 1,000 m3 per capita per year

  • AMERICA

    45%
    Water availability in America varies widely due to regional differences and issues. Some regions have ample freshwater resources from rivers and aquifers, while others face challenges such as drought, pollution, and overuse. Sustainable management practices are essential to address these issues and ensure reliable water access for communities, agriculture, and industry while protecting the environment.

  • EUROPE

    15.8
    Water availability in Europe varies across the continent. Generally, Northern and Western Europe have abundant freshwater resources, including rivers, lakes, and groundwater. However, Southern Europe faces challenges such as water scarcity due to arid climates and increasing demand. Sustainable water management practices, including conservation, infrastructure development, and cross-border cooperation, are crucial to ensure sufficient water supply for all European countries.

  • AUSTRALIA

    0.02%
    Australia faces water scarcity due to its arid climate. Surface water is limited, and groundwater overuse is a concern. Sustainable management, including desalination and recycling, is essential for reliable supply amid climate challenges and population growth.

  • ANTARTICA

    90%
    Antarctica holds about 70% of the world's freshwater, mainly in the form of ice. However, accessing this water is challenging due to the continent's harsh climate and remote location. Limited liquid water exists in lakes and streams, but overall, Antarctic water resources are difficult to utilize for practical purposes.

Major Causes Of Water Scarcity

Here are some of the causes of water scarcity.
*For more info click on the name*

  • CLIMATE CHANGE

    Climate change is exacerbating both water scarcity and water-related hazards, as rising temperatures disrupt precipitation patterns and the entire water cycle.

  • Natural calamities

    Natural calamities such as droughts and floods exacerbate water scarcity by depleting freshwater sources and contaminating existing water supplies, emphasizing the need for proactive water management and infrastructure resilience to ensure water security for communities.

  • POPULATION

    Water scarcity is a worldwide crisis and is caused by a combination of issues resulting from rapid population growth and urbanization, such as hydrological variabilities, overexploitation, and high human use (Jia et al., 2020).

  • WASTAGE OF WATER

    Water wastage exacerbates water crises by depleting limited freshwater resources, straining already stressed water systems, and worsening scarcity. Efficient water management, infrastructure improvements, and behavior changes are essential to mitigate wastage and ensure sustainable water availability.

  • FRESH WATER DEMAND

    As the demand for water continues to rise due to population growth, urbanisation, and industrialisation, the supply of freshwater sources remains limited.

  • LACK OF INFRASTRUCTURE

    Lack of infrastructure worsens water scarcity by hindering access to clean water and efficient distribution systems, particularly in rural areas. Investment in infrastructure development and maintenance is crucial to ensure equitable access to water resources and mitigate the impact of scarcity on communities.

Water Resource Management

Here are some of the ways to manage water resources.
*For more info click on the name*

  • RAIN WATER HARVESTING

    Rainwater harvesting (RWH) is the collection and storage of rain, rather than allowing it to run off. Rainwater is collected from a roof like surface and redirected to a tank, cistern, deep pit (well, shaft, or borehole), aquifer, or a reservoir with percolation, so that it seeps down and restores the ground water.

  • GROUND WATER RECHARGE

    Groundwater recharge is defined as the downward movement of water from the unsaturated (vadose) zone of soil or rock and into the groundwater saturated (phreatic) zone (Fetter, 2001). From: Ecological Engineering, 2020.

  • DRIP IRRIGATION

    Drip irrigation involves placing tubing with emitters on the ground along side the plants. The emitters slowly drip water into the soil at the root zone. Because moisture levels are kept at an optimal range, plant productivity and quality improve.

    Government campaigns for water include initiatives promoting water conservation, education on water-related issues, incentives for water-efficient practices, responses to droughts, protection of water quality, and community engagement. These campaigns aim to raise awareness, encourage behavior change, and mobilize action to address water challenges effectively.
